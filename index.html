Causality Analysis (To put in different page)

We’ve reached the final part of our analysis. Building up on our previous plots and analyses on genre and character types, we now want to acknowledge the reliability of our data comparisons. This is why we are performing a causality analysis.

The following histogram portrays the ethnicities of all actors in the movies dataset. We can observe first and foremost that caucasians represent the large majority of the actors, followed by african, and then trailing with other ethnicities in low minority.

The first scatter plot coupled with a probability density distribution tests the comparison of the number of different ethnicities in a movie and the revenue generated from the movie. What is observed is that as the number of ethnicities increases, the movie tends towards a higher revenue. However, not much can be concluded, since there is visibly a much larger amount of movies with less ethnicities than there is with over 3 ethnicities. Once the plot reaches 5 to 6 different ethnicities, the data becomes less reliable.

The second plot compares the movie rating to the number of different ethnicities. Again, by disregarding the outliers stemming from movies with 5 and 6 different ethnicities, we observe that the ratings maintain similar ratings, thus reinforcing that the number of diversity does not impact movie ratings.

Comparatively, box plots are presented for a more visual representation of the previous graphs. Since the distribution of ethnicities is very skewed, a Kruskal-Wallis test was conducted to fortify the analysis, and the p-value was XXXX. This allows to test if we can reject the null hypothesis: all ethnic groups have the same median revenue. We see that the ethnicities do not all have the same revenue, and this can be observed through the interactive box plot.

Movie rating versus different ethnicities was also compared through a box plot. We also conducted a Kruskal-Wallis test, and we came up with the following conclusion: different ethnicities do not have the same ratings, but we can observe that the repartition of ethnicities is skewed, as there are much much caucasians as opposed to other ethnicities. The Kruskal-Wallis test is non-sensitive to the distribution of the data, however it is a non-parametric test and only give us the information that at least one category doesn’t have the same median, but we do not know how many and which ones.
